Lessons from my data science internship

July 1, 2018

4 minute read

I’m not an expert in data science. I’ve just made a few mistakes. I’m sure I’ll make many more. But every mistake is a lesson learned, and I’d like to share those lessons here.

On data

  • Don’t try to guess what an abbreviation or code stands for unless you absolutely have to. Someone nearby probably knows.
  • Follow up on discrepancies in the data. You won’t be able to fix all of them—or even any of them—but you should at least note their existence.
  • Assumptions are dangerous, but also necessary. The worst assumptions are those you make without recognising them as assumptions.
  • SQL joins always take longer than you think.
  • Excel is fantastic for descriptive statistics. PivotTables may not be trendy but they are powerful.
  • Don’t accept the first bit of data you find; there might be something better out there.
  • When I was teaching in a university, I would always tell my students that they should graph their data whenever they can. I was right, but I still find myself forgetting to graph my data.
  • On this note, deciles and percentiles are a great way to graph data.
  • Scepticism of data is good; embrace it.
  • I underestimated the amount of transforming that goes into data wrangling.

On modelling

  • The fancy and cool statistical techniques are not always better than the basics.
  • Logistic regression is a powerful technique.
  • Don’t black box techniques when you should take the time to understand them.
  • Don’t take the time to understand techniques when you should black box them.
  • Dealing with multicollinearity in a logistic regression model just makes life easier, in terms of interpretability and dimension reduction.
  • Pay attention to what a 0 means for a particular variable in a logistic regression, since the effect of a 0 is part of the intercept. For example, if you’re modelling gender as 0/1, what does your 0-flag represent? What about age? More broadly, what does a point with a 0 for every attribute look like?
  • Pay attention to variables in which outliers can only be in one direction. Bounding the variable above or below, or even binning can help to deal with these.
  • The dummy variable you might introduce to flag missing data is very important.
  • I think I’m experiencing something like “model insecurity”. I model something and I get some figures to show * that it has predictive capacity, but I just don’t trust my own work.

Uhoh.jpg

On model evaluation

  • Before you begin modelling, write down how you’ll know you’ve got a “good model”.
  • Even more important than that, write down how you’ll know you’ve got a “good enough model”.
  • I need to be more comfortable working with unbalanced data sets, eg. data sets in which I’m predicting a binary outcome but one of the outcomes belongs to a small fraction of the data.
  • Working out the predictive capacity of your model is great, but you should be prepared to justify its predictive capacity (or lack thereof) to others.

On dimension reduction

  • I just don’t trust stepwise regression, or at least there’s something I’m not getting. It took out the flags I included for missing data, which had a huge impact on predictive accuracy. Despite that, it is fun to watch variables appear and disappear from the formula as the stepwise procedure is run.
  • Human beings can and should have input in which variables are important. That being said, I looked at some variables and thought, “Wow, there’s no way that will be predictive of anything.” I was wrong.

On R

  • Make sure your factors are declared as factors. It’s an easy thing to forget.
  • …but don’t declare a continuous variable as a factor unless you want to watch R struggle with thousands of levels.
  • There’s a difference between & and &&.

On corporate life

  • A one hour meeting with a stakeholder is not enough to get their perspective on the problem. I wonder if lots of 30-second meetings would be better?
  • Personal brand is important. No, I’m serious. I know that when you go to university seminars on personal brand you might think it’s all a bit silly—I certainly did—but it actually matters. Even just the way you act around the office is part of that personal brand.
  • Tattoos aren’t as taboo as they used to be!
  • Presentation skills are extremely important, and potentially a strong point for PhD graduates. I learned so much from asking people to catch up over a coffee. It is always worth the $5, and people rarely say no.
